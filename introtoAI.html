How AI Works

Learn the core ideas behind generative AI and large language models.

Part 1

What Is a Large Language Model?

Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, and articles — using a process called self-supervised learning.

The model learns to predict what word or token is most likely to come next. Over time, it builds a statistical map of language patterns.

Part 2

How AI Generates Responses

When a user enters a prompt, the model does not search for one stored answer. Instead, it generates a response token by token based on probabilities, context, and patterns learned during training.

This is why AI can sound fluent and helpful even though it is predicting language rather than thinking like a human.

Part 3

Why the Output Can Vary

The same prompt can produce slightly different responses because AI systems sample from likely next-token options.

Small changes in wording, context, or system settings can shift the final output.

Part 1

Hallucinations and Inaccuracies

AI can produce information that sounds confident but is inaccurate, unsupported, or completely made up.

These errors are often called hallucinations.

Bias and Missing Context

AI systems reflect the strengths and weaknesses of their training data. That means they can reproduce social bias, miss nuance, or oversimplify complex issues.

A polished response is not automatically a fair or complete one.
