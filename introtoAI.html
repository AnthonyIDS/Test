How AI Works

What Is a Large Language Model?

Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, articles — using a process called self-supervised learning. The model learns to predict the next word in a sentence, billions of times, until it builds a rich statistical map of human language and knowledge.

Think of it less like a database and more like a very well-read collaborator who learned everything by reading — and can now converse, write, reason, and create.

Key insight: The model doesn't "look up" answers. It generates them — which is both its power and its weakness.

How Does It Generate Text?

When you send a prompt, the model doesn't retrieve a stored answer — it generates a response token by token (roughly word by word), sampling from a probability distribution at each step.

Responses can vary slightly even for the same prompt because the model is generating based on probabilities, context, and settings.

What AI Gets Wrong

AI can sound confident even when it is wrong. Because it generates responses based on patterns in data rather than true understanding, it can produce inaccurate facts, made-up sources, biased ideas, or incomplete explanations.

This is sometimes called a hallucination — when the AI presents false or unsupported information as if it were true.

AI also reflects the limitations of the data it was trained on. That means it can reproduce bias, miss important context, oversimplify complex issues, or present outdated information.

Key insight: Just because AI sounds polished does not mean it is accurate, fair, or complete.

Good AI use requires verification. Users should fact-check claims, review sources carefully, and apply their own judgment before trusting or submitting AI-generated content.

Section 3 of 3

Ethics & Integrity

Using AI responsibly means thinking about more than whether it works. It also means considering fairness, transparency, privacy, and academic integrity.

In academic settings, the core principle remains the same: your submitted work should represent your own thinking and learning. If AI is used, students should follow course policies, disclose use when required, and avoid presenting AI-generated work as entirely their own.

Ethical AI use also includes protecting sensitive information, recognizing the potential for bias, and understanding that AI tools can have real social, environmental, and human costs.

Key insight: Responsible AI use is not just about what the tool can do — it is about what you should do.

Before using AI, ask: Is it allowed? Am I using it to support my learning rather than replace it? Am I being honest and transparent about how I used it?
