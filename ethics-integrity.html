Academic Integrity & AI

The core principle hasn't changed: your submitted work should represent your own thinking and learning. What's changed is the landscape.

Key questions to ask yourself:

  • Has your instructor permitted AI use for this assignment?
  • Are you using AI to enhance your thinking or to replace it?
  • Are you disclosing AI assistance where required?
  • Are you verifying AI-generated content before submitting it as fact?

Different instructors, different policies. Always check. Silence doesn't mean permission. When in doubt, ask directly — professors generally appreciate proactive honesty.

How to Disclose AI Use

Transparency is the ethical foundation. If you use AI in your work, disclose it — even if not explicitly required.

Citation styles are evolving, but common approaches include:

APA: OpenAI. (2024). ChatGPT (GPT-4) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com

MLA: "Prompt text here." ChatGPT, GPT-4 version, OpenAI, 15 Jan. 2024, chat.openai.com.

Beyond formal citation, a methods note in your paper is often the most meaningful form of disclosure: "I used Claude to help brainstorm counterarguments in section 2, which I then researched and rewrote."

The next section introduces a dedicated AI disclosure tool — SAID — that makes this process structured and easy.

The Hidden Costs of AI

AI has costs that aren't visible when you're chatting with it.

  • Environmental: Training a large AI model can emit as much CO₂ as five cars driven for a year. Each query consumes significant water for data center cooling.
  • Labor: Data labeling and content moderation relies heavily on low-paid workers, often exposed to harmful content.
  • Economic displacement: AI is automating tasks across many industries — a reality with complex effects on labor markets.
  • Privacy: Inputs to commercial AI tools may be used for training. Avoid sharing sensitive personal, medical, or confidential information unless you've reviewed the privacy policy.
