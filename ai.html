Section One

What Is a Large Language Model?

Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, articles — using a process called self-supervised learning. The model learns to predict the next word in a sentence, billions of times, until it builds a rich statistical map of human language and knowledge.

Think of it less like a database and more like a very well-read collaborator who learned everything by reading — and can now converse, write, reason, and create.

Key insight: The model doesn't "look up" answers. It generates them — which is both its power and its weakness.

Section Two

How Does It Generate Text?

When you send a prompt, the model doesn't retrieve a stored answer — it generates a response token by token (roughly word by word), sampling from a probability distribution at each step.

  • Responses can vary slightly even for the same prompt.
  • The model can be creative (or wrong) in novel ways.
  • There's a "temperature" setting controlling randomness.

High temperature → more creative, less predictable

Low temperature → more focused and deterministic

This also explains why you can get different answers by rephrasing the same question — you're nudging the probability distribution.

Section Three

The Context Window

Every AI model has a context window — the maximum amount of text it can "see" at once. Think of it as working memory. Anything outside it is invisible to the model.

Modern models have grown dramatically: from ~4,000 tokens in 2020 to 200,000+ tokens today. This allows AI to analyze full books, long codebases, or extended conversations.

1 token ≈ ¾ of a word in English. A typical college essay is ~750–1,500 tokens.

Practical implication: In a long conversation, the model may "forget" things said early on — they've scrolled out of the window.
