What Are Hallucinations?

AI "hallucinations" are when a model confidently generates false information. Unlike a human who might say "I don't know," an LLM is trained to always produce plausible-sounding text — so when it doesn't know, it invents.

Common examples:

  • Fake citations and DOIs for academic papers
  • Incorrect dates, statistics, or historical facts
  • Made-up quotes attributed to real people
  • Nonexistent laws, court cases, or products

The danger: the false information often sounds highly credible and specific. Always verify claims that matter.

Lateral Reading: The Expert Move

Professional fact-checkers use lateral reading — instead of reading deeply into one source, they immediately open new tabs to check what others say about that source.

When verifying AI output:

  1. Identify the specific claim (not just the topic)
  2. Search for the original source — don't trust AI's citation without checking
  3. Consult multiple independent sources — especially authoritative ones
  4. Check for recency — AI knowledge has a cutoff date

Library databases like JSTOR, PubMed, and your university's subscriptions are strong resources for verification.

Bias in AI Systems

AI models reflect the biases in their training data — and training data is human-generated text, which carries society's historical prejudices and imbalances.

Types of bias to watch for:

  • Representation bias: underrepresentation of certain groups, languages, or perspectives
  • Historical bias: training on old data encoding old norms
  • Confirmation bias: the model may agree with you even when you're wrong
  • Cultural bias: defaulting to Western, English-language perspectives

This doesn't mean AI is useless — it means you need to critically evaluate outputs, especially for topics involving people, policy, and culture.
